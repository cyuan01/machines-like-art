Machines Like Art Too

Overview

With advances in machine learning comes an opportunity to utilize this technology beyond more typical applications such as facial recognition or insurance estimates. At Cuseum, we decided to bring the latest technologies in computer vision to the world of art. While many works of art are highly written about with paragraphs of descriptive information, even more works are left with little to no information. But what if we handed this description job to computers? Just as different curators may have differing opinions on a piece of art, what differing opinions will various machine learning programs have? We took a sample of works from 3 different museums and analyzed what descriptive tags different machine learning programs produced for each piece.

What is Computer Vision & Machine Learning?


Machine Learning is simply the learning of a computer program from experience. In general, a program will learn from a large "training" dataset, which in the case of machine vision consists of images or parts of images, and their respective labels. In the training dataset these labels are usually created and checked by humans to ensure 100% accuracy of say an image of an apple and the label "apple". And scaled out to millions of images and labels, these datasets will be able to teach programs how to "see". Programs can then pick out patterns in color, spacial layout, and various other features for each label using these pre defined images and labels to ultimatley make its own predictions on new images. So by analyzing patterns of a million different apple pictures labeled "apple", a program would theoretically be able to analyze a new picture of an apple and label it "apple" if the patterns matched.
